101.1

Why accounting exists: stakeholders & the accounting equation

Before any debit or credit makes sense, you need to know who the numbers are for and the one equation that can never break.

Learn

A business does hundreds of things a week — sells, buys, borrows, pays people. Accounting is the discipline of turning that messy stream of events into a small set of trustworthy numbers that outsiders can rely on. The reason it exists is simple: the people who need to make decisions about a business usually can't see inside it.

The information gap

The owner who runs a company day-to-day knows roughly how it's doing. But a bank deciding whether to lend, an investor deciding whether to buy in, or a tax authority deciding what's owed — none of them live inside the business. Accounting closes that gap. It produces a standardized report so a stranger can judge the company's health without being there. Standardization is the whole point: if every business invented its own format, the numbers couldn't be compared or trusted.

Who the numbers are for: stakeholders

A stakeholder is anyone with an interest in how the business performs. They split into two camps, and this split drives almost everything in the curriculum:

External — investors, lenders, suppliers, tax authorities, regulators, customers. They get standardized financial statements. (This is financial accounting.)
Internal — managers and employees who run operations. They get detailed, custom reports to make decisions. (This is managerial accounting — course 105.)

Different audiences, different rules. External reporting follows shared standards (e.g. GAAP / IFRS) precisely because outsiders must be able to trust and compare it.

The accounting equation: the invariant

Everything a business owns had to be paid for somehow — and there are only two sources of that funding: money lent to the business, and money the owners put in (or left in). That single fact is the accounting equation:

Assets  =  Liabilities  +  Equity
TermPlain EnglishExamples
AssetsWhat the business owns / controls and expects to benefit from.Cash, inventory, equipment, money owed to it (receivables).
LiabilitiesWhat the business owes to outsiders — claims by creditors.Bank loans, unpaid bills (payables), wages owed.
EquityThe owners' residual claim — what's left after creditors are paid.Owner investment, retained earnings.

Read it as a sentence: the resources a company controls (assets) equal the claims against those resources, split between outsiders (liabilities) and owners (equity). Equity is deliberately the leftover — rearrange to Equity = Assets − Liabilities and you get the owners' true stake, also called net worth or net assets.

Systems analogy — a conservation law. If you've done any physics or engineering, treat the equation like conservation of mass: nothing enters or leaves the system without being accounted for on both sides of a boundary. Every transaction is a balanced flow. Buy a $10k machine with cash? One asset (equipment) goes up $10k, another asset (cash) goes down $10k — the books still balance. Take a $10k loan? Assets (cash) +$10k and liabilities (loan) +$10k — still balanced. The equation is the invariant the system maintains after every event. Double-entry bookkeeping (101.5) is just the mechanism that enforces this invariant.

Why "it always balances" is powerful

Because the equation can never legitimately break, it doubles as an error-check. If you record every transaction and the two sides don't match, you know something is wrong — a missing or lopsided entry. This self-checking property is why the system has survived ~500 years essentially unchanged.

  • The balance sheet (101.2) is literally a snapshot of this equation at a moment in time.
  • Each transaction keeps it balanced by touching at least two items.
  • Profit the business keeps flows into equity as retained earnings — linking the income statement (101.3) back to the equation.
