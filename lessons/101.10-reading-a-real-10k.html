101.10

Reading a Real 10-K: Tying the Four Statements Together

Everything from 101.1–101.9 was practice for this: opening a company's annual filing and reading it the way the statements were designed to be read — as one interlocking model, seams and all.

Learn

1. What a 10-K is, and where things live

The Form 10-K is the comprehensive annual report every U.S. public company files with the SEC (on EDGAR, free, within 60–90 days of fiscal year-end depending on size). It is the audited, legally accountable version of the company's story — far meatier than the glossy “annual report” mailed to shareholders. The map:

ItemContentsWhy you'd read it
Item 1 — BusinessWhat the company does, segments, competitionFirst read for any unfamiliar company
Item 1A — Risk FactorsEverything that could go wrong (legally vetted)Skim for the risks that are specific, not boilerplate
Item 3 — Legal ProceedingsMaterial litigationContingent liabilities preview
Item 7 — MD&AManagement's Discussion & Analysis: results explained, liquidity, trendsManagement's own narrative of the numbers — read it against the numbers
Item 8 — Financial StatementsThe four audited statements + notes + auditor's reportThe destination; everything else is commentary
Item 9A — ControlsInternal control over financial reportingMaterial weaknesses disclosed here

Two parts of Item 8 deserve special respect. The notes to the financial statements are where the real information density lives: accounting policies (which revenue recognition method? what depreciation lives?), lease obligations, debt maturities, segment detail, commitments and contingencies. The face of the statements is a summary; the notes are the contract. And the auditor's report: an unqualified (clean) opinion says the statements present fairly, in all material respects, per GAAP — it is not a guarantee against fraud or an endorsement of the stock. Deviations (qualified opinions, going-concern doubt, adverse opinions) are rare and serious.

2. The articulation seams — your built-in audit

The four statements share numbers at fixed seams. Checking them takes two minutes and proves you're reading one coherent model:

  • Net income (income statement) → start of the cash flow statement (indirect method) and into the retained-earnings column of the statement of stockholders' equity.
  • Ending retained earnings (equity statement) → equity section of the balance sheet.
  • Ending cash (cash flow statement: beginning cash + CFO + CFI + CFF) → the cash line of the balance sheet.
  • Beginning balances of this year's statements = ending balances of last year's (comparative columns let you verify).
Worked example — seam checks on a retailer (in $M)

Income statement: revenue 1,840; net income 95. Equity statement: beginning RE 220 + 95 − dividends 30 = ending RE 285. Balance sheet: retained earnings line shows 285 ✓. Cash flow statement: beginning cash 64 + CFO 118 + CFI (−85) + CFF (−22) = ending cash 75; balance sheet cash line shows 75 ✓. The model closes. If a seam doesn't tie, you've misread a line (or found a restatement in progress).

3. Reading for quality, not just quantity

The statements tied together also let you interrogate earnings quality — whether accrual net income is backed by cash:

  • Net income up, operating cash flow down — over several years, the classic warning. Accrual income that never becomes cash suggests aggressive revenue recognition (101.7's fraud chapter) or deteriorating collections.
  • Receivables growing much faster than revenue — sales are being booked that customers haven't paid for; channel stuffing lives here.
  • Inventory growing faster than sales — future markdowns or write-offs in waiting.
  • One-time items recurring — “restructuring charges” every single year are simply costs.
  • Auditor or control red flags — a going-concern paragraph, a material weakness in Item 9A, or an auditor change mid-controversy.

5. A reading order that works

For a first pass on an unfamiliar company, resist reading front-to-back: (1) Item 1 for the business model; (2) Item 8 — statements first, then seam checks, then the revenue recognition and debt notes; (3) Item 7 MD&A, asking where management's story and the numbers disagree; (4) Item 1A for the two or three risks that are specific to this firm; (5) the auditor's report and Item 9A last, as a hygiene check. An hour spent this way beats a day of front-to-back reading.
