101.2

The balance sheet: assets, liabilities, equity

If the accounting equation is the law, the balance sheet is the photograph that proves it held at a single instant.

Learn

In 101.1 you met the invariant: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. The balance sheet (also called the statement of financial position) is simply that equation written out in full, line by line, as of one specific date. It answers: at this instant, what does the business control, what does it owe, and what's left for the owners?

A snapshot, not a movie

This is the single most important framing. The balance sheet is a point-in-time statement — "as of Dec 31, 2025." It's a stock, not a flow. The income statement (101.3) and cash flow statement (101.4) cover a period ("for the year ended…") — they're flows. Mixing these up is the most common beginner error.

Systems analogy — state vs. events. Think of the business as a stateful system. The balance sheet is a state dump: the exact contents of every register at one clock tick. The income statement is the event log of what happened between two ticks. You reconstruct the new state by replaying the events onto the old state — which is exactly how retained earnings link the two statements.

The three blocks — and the ordering rule

Each side is grouped, and within each group items are conventionally ordered by liquidity (how fast they turn into, or consume, cash):

Assets — resources the business controls. Split by a one-year horizon:

  • Current assets — expected to become cash or be used up within one year/operating cycle: cash, accounts receivable, inventory, prepaid expenses. Listed most-liquid first (cash at top).
  • Non-current (long-term) assets — used over many years: property, plant & equipment (PP&E), long-term investments, intangibles (patents, goodwill).

Liabilities — claims of outsiders (creditors). Same split by when they come due:

  • Current liabilities — due within one year: accounts payable, accrued wages, short-term debt, the current portion of long-term debt.
  • Non-current liabilities — due beyond one year: long-term loans, bonds payable, lease obligations.

Equity — the owners' residual claim. For a corporation, typically:

  • Contributed capital (e.g. common stock) — what owners paid in.
  • Retained earnings — cumulative profits kept in the business rather than paid out as dividends. This is the hinge that connects the income statement back to the balance sheet.
Total Assets  =  Total Liabilities  +  Total Equity   (always)

What the structure reveals: liquidity

Splitting current from non-current isn't bureaucratic — it lets a reader judge whether the business can pay its near-term bills:

  • Working capital = Current assets − Current liabilities. The cushion of short-term resources over short-term obligations.
  • Current ratio = Current assets ÷ Current liabilities. A quick solvency check; comfortably above 1 means near-term obligations are covered by near-term resources.
