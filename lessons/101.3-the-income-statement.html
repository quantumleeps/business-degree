101.3

The income statement: revenue, expenses, net income

The balance sheet is the photograph; the income statement is the flow meter — it measures how fast the business created (or destroyed) owner value over a stretch of time.

Learn

In 101.2 you saw the balance sheet: a stock, a state dump as of one date. The income statement (also called the P&L, profit-and-loss statement, or statement of operations) is its complement: a flow statement. Its heading always reads "for the period ended…" — a quarter, a year — and it answers: over that stretch, how much value did operations generate for the owners? The answer is net income: revenues minus expenses.

Rate vs. state

An engineer's framing: if equity is the level in a tank, net income is the net inflow rate integrated over the period. Revenue is flow in, expenses are flow out, and the period's accumulated difference gets dumped into the retained earnings register on the balance sheet when the period closes. That's why the two statements can never disagree — the flow literally integrates into the stock:

Ending RE  =  Beginning RE  +  Net income  −  Dividends
Systems analogy — the derivative of equity. If the balance sheet is the system state at clock ticks t₀ and t₁, the income statement is (most of) the diff between them, attributed to operations. Equity can also change from owner transactions — paid-in capital and dividends — so the income statement isolates the part of Δequity the business earned, as opposed to the part the owners moved in or out themselves. Mixing those two up is how bad dashboards get built.

The multi-step structure

GAAP income statements for most real companies use the multi-step format: a cascade of subtotals that separates how good the product economics are from how expensive the organization is from how the company is financed and taxed. Read it top to bottom:

LineWhat it is
Revenue (the "top line")Value of goods/services delivered in the period — not cash collected. Also called sales or turnover.
− Cost of goods sold (COGS)The direct cost of the units actually sold: materials, direct labor, factory overhead. Inventory still on the shelf is an asset, not COGS.
= Gross profitWhat the product itself earns before running the company. Tests the unit economics.
− Operating expensesSG&A (selling, general & administrative: salaries, rent, marketing), R&D, and depreciation & amortization (D&A) on operating assets.
= Operating income (EBIT)Earnings before interest and taxes: profit from the core business, ignoring how it's financed.
± Non-operating itemsInterest expense/income, gains or losses on selling assets — things outside the core business.
= Pre-tax income (EBT)What the tax authority gets to look at.
− Income tax expenseRoughly pre-tax income × effective tax rate.
= Net income (the "bottom line")What's left for the owners. This is the number that flows to retained earnings.

Each subtotal earns its keep as a margin — the subtotal divided by revenue, so businesses of different sizes become comparable:

Gross margin = GP ÷ Revenue  ·  Operating margin = EBIT ÷ Revenue  ·  Net margin = NI ÷ Revenue

Margins localize problems the way subtotals localize bugs: a falling gross margin points at pricing or production cost; a healthy gross margin with a sagging operating margin points at overhead (SG&A bloat); a gap between operating and net margin points at debt load or taxes.

What counts, and when: the accrual rules

The single most important — and least intuitive — fact about the income statement: revenue is not cash received, and expense is not cash paid. GAAP uses accrual accounting, governed by two principles (we'll go deep in 101.7; here's the core):

  • Revenue recognition principle — record revenue in the period you earn it, i.e. when you deliver the good or perform the service, regardless of when cash moves. A customer prepayment is not revenue yet (it's a liability, deferred revenue — you owe them delivery). A delivery not yet paid for is revenue (and an asset, accounts receivable).
  • Matching (expense recognition) principle — record expenses in the same period as the revenues they helped generate. COGS hits when the unit sells, not when you bought the materials. Wages earned by employees in December are December's expense even if payday is in January.

Why bother? Because the income statement is trying to measure economic performance per period, and cash timing is noisy — customers pay late, suppliers get paid early, machines are bought once and used for a decade. Accrual accounting filters the cash-timing noise out of the performance signal. (The cash-timing truth still matters — that's the statement of cash flows, lesson 101.4.)

Depreciation: an expense with no cash attached

The matching principle's most famous consequence. Buy a $200,000 machine that will help produce revenue for 10 years: expensing $200,000 in year one would crater that year's income and flatter the next nine. Instead the cost is capitalized (recorded as an asset) and then depreciated — allocated as an expense across the periods it serves. Straight-line: $200,000 ÷ 10 = $20,000/year of depreciation expense, even though the cash left long ago. Amortization is the same idea for intangibles. This is why analysts compute EBITDA (EBIT + D&A) — a non-GAAP measure that strips out these non-cash, financing-and-history-laden charges to approximate operating cash generation. Useful, but treat it with suspicion: machines really do wear out, and EBITDA pretends they don't.

Worked example: Cedar & Bolt Co., year ended Dec 31

Cedar & Bolt machines and sells desk hardware. Raw facts for the year:

  • Delivered 10,000 units at $50 each. Collected $420,000 in cash; the remaining $80,000 is still owed by customers.
  • Also received a $15,000 customer deposit for units to be delivered next year.
  • Each unit sold cost $30 to produce. Also bought $40,000 of extra materials still sitting in inventory at year end.
  • Paid salaries, rent, and marketing of $78,000 in cash; employees earned another $2,000 of wages in December, payable in January.
  • Owns one machine, bought earlier for $200,000, straight-line over 10 years, no salvage value.
  • Owes 10% interest on a $100,000 bank loan; effective tax rate 25%; 50,000 common shares outstanding.

Apply the recognition rules before touching the statement: revenue = 10,000 × $50 = $500,000 (delivery happened; the uncollected $80,000 doesn't reduce it, and the $15,000 deposit doesn't increase it — nothing was delivered for it). COGS = 10,000 × $30 = $300,000 (the $40,000 of unsold materials stays on the balance sheet as inventory). SG&A = $78,000 + $2,000 accrued = $80,000 (matching: December wages belong to December). Depreciation = $200,000 ÷ 10 = $20,000 (no cash this year). Interest = $10,000.

Margins: gross 200/500 = 40%; operating 100/500 = 20%; net 67.5/500 = 13.5%. EBITDA = 100,000 + 20,000 = $120,000. Basic EPS = 67,500 ÷ 50,000 shares = $1.35. And notice the accrual gap: cash in from customers was 420,000 + 15,000 = $435,000, yet revenue is $500,000 — the difference lives on the balance sheet as receivables and deferred revenue.

Articulation: closing into the balance sheet

At period end, net income doesn't vanish — it closes into retained earnings, the equity account from 101.2. If Cedar & Bolt started the year with retained earnings of $50,000 and paid $17,500 of dividends, ending RE = 50,000 + 67,500 − 17,500 = $100,000. The asset side moved in lockstep along the way (cash, receivables, inventory, accumulated depreciation), so A = L + E still holds. Accountants call this linkage articulation: the statements are views of one consistent underlying state machine, not independent reports.

Edge cases and vocabulary worth keeping

  • Single-step vs. multi-step. A single-step statement just lumps all revenues and gains, subtracts all expenses and losses, and reports net income — legal under GAAP, common for simple firms, but it hides gross and operating margin. Multi-step adds the subtotals.
  • Operating vs. non-operating is about the business, not the item. Interest expense is non-operating for a retailer but operating revenue for a bank — lending is a bank's core business. Classify by relationship to the core operations.
  • Earnings per share (EPS). Basic EPS = (net income − preferred dividends) ÷ weighted-average common shares outstanding. Required on the face of public-company income statements; it's net income restated per unit of ownership.
  • Revenue ≠ quality. Two firms with identical net income can have wildly different mixes (high-margin shrinking core vs. low-margin growth). The cascade exists precisely so you can see the mix.
  • Watch the period. Comparing a quarterly P&L to an annual one, or a flow (net income) to a stock (cash balance), is a unit error — like comparing watts to joules.
