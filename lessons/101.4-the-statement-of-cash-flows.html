101.4

The statement of cash flows: operating, investing, financing

Net income is a modeled estimate; cash is a conserved quantity — this statement is the mass balance that reconciles the one number a business cannot fake running out of.

Learn

You now have a snapshot (the balance sheet, 101.2) and an accrual event log (the income statement, 101.3). Why a third statement? Because the income statement deliberately does not track cash. Under accrual accounting, revenue is recorded when earned and expenses when incurred — not when money moves. That makes net income a better measure of economic performance, but it means a company can report healthy profits while its bank account drains to zero. Businesses don't die of low net income; they die when they can't make payroll. The statement of cash flows (CFS) exists to answer the question the other two statements dodge: where did the cash actually come from, and where did it actually go?

Profitable but insolvent — the failure mode

Imagine a fast-growing machine shop. It books $500k of revenue this year — all on 90-day credit terms — while paying cash up front for steel, wages, and two new CNC machines. The income statement shows a solid profit: revenue earned, expenses matched. The bank account shows a catastrophe: cash went out months before any came in. If the shop can't bridge the gap, it goes under while profitable. This is not exotic — running out of working capital during growth is one of the most common ways otherwise-sound businesses fail. The CFS is the instrument that makes the gap between accrual profit and cash visible before it becomes fatal.

Engineering framing — a mass balance on cash. Cash is a conserved quantity: it cannot be created by an accounting estimate, only moved across the system boundary. The CFS is exactly a control-volume balance: accumulation = inflows − outflows, integrated over the period. Beginning cash plus every flow across the boundary must equal ending cash — to the dollar, with no plug terms. And like a good energy audit, the statement doesn't just total the flows; it partitions them by mechanism, so you can see which subsystem is generating or consuming cash. Net income involves judgment calls (depreciation schedules, revenue timing, estimates of bad debts). The cash balance is checkable against a bank statement. That's why analysts treat the CFS as the hardest statement to manipulate.

The governing identity

Every cash flow during the period is classified into exactly one of three activities — the partition is exhaustive and mutually exclusive:

ΔCash  =  CFO + CFI + CFF
Beginning cash + CFO + CFI + CFF = Ending cash
CFO = cash flow from operating · CFI = investing · CFF = financing

Each section answers a different question about the business as a system:

  • Operating (CFO) — does the core engine generate cash? Receipts from customers; payments to suppliers and employees; and — a convention to memorize — interest paid, interest received, and dividends received are all operating under US GAAP, because they run through the income statement. Income taxes paid are operating too.
  • Investing (CFI) — what is the business doing to its productive capacity? Purchases of property, plant & equipment (capital expenditures, "capex"), proceeds from selling long-term assets, purchases and sales of investment securities, loans made to others.
  • Financing (CFF) — how is the business funded, and what is it returning to its funders? Borrowing and repaying principal on debt, issuing stock, buying back stock, and paying dividends (paid = financing, even though received = operating).
Classification gotchas that show up on every exam:
  • Interest paid is operating, not financing (US GAAP). Only the principal of a loan flows through financing. Rationale: interest expense is on the income statement, so its cash twin stays in operating. (IFRS allows either; we use the GAAP rule.)
  • Selling an asset shows the full proceeds, not the book value. Sell a machine carried at $40k for $52k cash → investing shows a $52k inflow. The $12k gain is an income-statement artifact, not a separate cash flow (more on this below).
  • Depreciation is nowhere on the CFS as a flow. No cash moves when you depreciate. It appears only as a reconciliation add-back in the indirect method — which trips people into thinking it's a "source of cash." It isn't; it's the removal of a non-cash expense.
  • Buying inventory is operating, buying a machine is investing. The one-year/operating-cycle logic from the balance sheet carries over: working-capital churn is operating; long-term capacity is investing.
  • Non-cash transactions (buy a building by issuing stock, convert debt to equity) appear in none of the three sections — they're disclosed in a supplementary note instead. Nothing crossed the cash boundary.

The indirect method: net income → CFO

There are two ways to compute the operating section. The direct method lists actual cash receipts and payments (collected from customers, paid to suppliers, paid to employees…). The indirect method — used by the overwhelming majority of companies — starts from net income and repairs it: undo everything accrual accounting did that didn't involve cash. Three repair categories:

1. Add back non-cash expenses. Depreciation and amortization (D&A) reduced net income but moved no cash this period (the cash left back when the asset was bought — and was recorded in investing then). Add them back.

2. Remove gains, add back losses on asset sales. Sell the $40k machine for $52k: net income includes a $12k gain, but the actual cash event is the $52k of proceeds, which belongs in investing. If you left the gain in operating and the proceeds in investing, you'd double-count $12k. So subtract gains (and symmetrically, add back losses) from net income.

3. Adjust for working-capital changes. Don't memorize the signs — derive them. Each operating accrual has a partner balance-sheet account that absorbs the timing difference between income and cash. Take receivables: ending AR = beginning AR + revenue billed − cash collected, so

Cash collected = Revenue − ΔAR
(ΔAR = ending − beginning; AR up means some revenue wasn't collected)

Same algebra for the purchasing pipeline: purchases = COGS + ΔInventory (you bought what you sold, plus whatever stocked the shelves), and cash paid = purchases − ΔAP (whatever suppliers financed, you didn't pay yet):

Cash paid to suppliers = COGS + ΔInventory − ΔAP

Stack these up and the general rule falls out:

CFO = Net income + non-cash expenses − gains (+ losses)
− Δ(operating assets) + Δ(operating liabilities)
  • Operating asset up (AR, inventory, prepaid) → cash is tied up there → subtract the increase.
  • Operating asset down → cash was released → add.
  • Operating liability up (AP, accrued wages, taxes payable) → someone else is floating you → add.
  • Operating liability down → you paid old obligations → subtract.

One more wrinkle: even companies that use the direct method must also present this net-income-to-CFO reconciliation under US GAAP — which is why almost everyone just publishes the indirect method and is done with it. Both methods produce the identical CFO; they differ only in presentation. Investing and financing sections are always direct — a plain list of cash flows.

The mass balance closes: 25,000 + 84,000 − 30,000 − 25,000 = 54,000, exactly the cash line on the ending balance sheet. Identical — as it must be; the two methods are different decompositions of the same conserved flow.

Articulation, free cash flow, and reading the signature

The three statements form a closed system — accountants call it articulation: the income statement explains the change in retained earnings between two balance sheets; the CFS explains the change in the cash line between the same two balance sheets. Given any two consecutive balance sheets and the income statement, the CFS is (mostly) derivable, which is exactly what we just did for Maple Tools.

Analysts immediately compute one derived metric from the CFS:

Free cash flow (FCF) = CFO − capital expenditures
cash the business generates after maintaining/expanding its asset base — what's genuinely available to creditors and owners

For Maple Tools: FCF = 84,000 − 45,000 = $39,000. (Definitions vary at the edges — some use net capex — but CFO − capex is the baseline form.)

Finally, the signs of the three sections form a lifecycle signature you can read at a glance, before looking at a single line item:

CFOCFICFFTypical story
−−+Startup: operations burn cash, heavy investment, kept alive by outside capital.
+−+Growth: the engine works but can't yet fund the expansion; still raising.
+−−Mature: self-funding; investing in maintenance; returning cash via dividends, buybacks, debt paydown.
−+−Distress: operations bleeding, selling assets to pay creditors.

Maple Tools reads (+, −, −): a healthy mature firm — operations generated $84k, of which $30k went into (net) capacity and $25k went back to its funders, with $29k left to pile up in the tank. Explore the full 8-combination space in the third visual below.
