101.5

Double-entry bookkeeping: debits, credits, T-accounts

Debits and credits are the grammar of accounting — every statement you studied in 101.2–101.4 was assembled one balanced entry at a time.

Learn

So far you've read the finished products: the balance sheet, the income statement, the statement of cash flows. This lesson is about the recording system that produces them. Double-entry bookkeeping is a five-century-old convention (Luca Pacioli described it in print in 1494) with one organizing idea: every transaction is recorded in at least two accounts, and the debits must equal the credits in every entry.

Why every transaction has two sides

The accounting equation, Assets = Liabilities + Equity, must hold after every transaction — not just at year end. That is only possible if each transaction is recorded in a way that keeps both sides equal. Consider what a single transaction can do:

  • Borrow cash from a bank → an asset (Cash) goes up and a liability (Notes Payable) goes up. Both sides rise by the same amount.
  • Buy equipment with cash → one asset (Equipment) goes up and another asset (Cash) goes down. The left side reshuffles; totals unchanged.
  • Pay off a supplier → an asset (Cash) goes down and a liability (Accounts Payable) goes down. Both sides fall together.

In every case, at least two accounts move. Double-entry bookkeeping doesn't impose this duality — it records it. The bookkeeping rule "total debits = total credits for every entry" is the mechanical expression of the equation holding, and it gives the system a built-in arithmetic check: if the debits and credits you've recorded don't add up to the same total, you know with certainty that an error exists somewhere.

Debit and credit: left and right — nothing more

This is where most beginners go wrong, so let's get it exactly right:

Definition. A debit (Dr.) is an entry on the left side of an account. A credit (Cr.) is an entry on the right side of an account. That is the entire definition. Debit does not mean increase, decrease, good, or bad — and neither does credit. Whether a debit increases or decreases an account depends entirely on which type of account it is.

The everyday meanings will mislead you. Your bank says it "credits your account" when you deposit money — but that's the bank's bookkeeping: your deposit is a liability to the bank (it owes you that money), and liabilities increase with credits. From your own books' perspective, the same deposit is a debit to your Cash account. Same event, opposite labels, because the two parties hold mirror-image positions.

The expanded accounting equation generates the rules

Equity isn't one undifferentiated lump. It changes through four channels: owners put capital in (contributed capital), the business earns revenues, it incurs expenses, and it pays dividends. Writing those out:

Assets = Liabilities + Contributed Capital + Retained Earnings + Revenues − Expenses − Dividends the expanded accounting equation

Now move the two negative terms to the left side so that everything carries a plus sign:

Assets + Expenses + Dividends  =  Liabilities + Contributed Capital + Retained Earnings + Revenues every account on the left increases with a debit; every account on the right increases with a credit

This rearrangement is the rulebook. The convention is: accounts on the left side of this equation are debit-normal — they increase with debits (left-side entries) and decrease with credits. Accounts on the right side are credit-normal — they increase with credits and decrease with debits. Nothing needs to be memorized account-by-account; the rules all fall out of where the account sits in the rearranged equation.

Mnemonic — DEA / LER. Dividends, Expenses, Assets increase with debits; Liabilities, Equity, Revenues increase with credits. (Some courses teach the same thing as "DEAD CLIC": Debits increase Expenses, Assets, Dividends; Credits increase Liabilities, Income, Capital.)

Pause on why revenues and expenses land where they do. Revenues increase retained earnings — they sit on the equity side, so they grow with credits, like equity. Expenses decrease retained earnings; a deduction from a credit-normal item behaves like its opposite, so expenses are debit-normal. Dividends, likewise a reduction of retained earnings, are debit-normal too. This is why "debiting an expense" — which sounds like jargon — is just the standard way of saying the expense grew.

Normal balances

An account's normal balance is the side on which it increases — and the side where its balance ordinarily ends up:

Watch out for names that lie about their type. Unearned Revenue is a liability (cash received for work not yet performed — you owe the customer the service), so it's credit-normal like any liability, not because of the word "revenue." Prepaid Rent is an asset (a right to future use), not an expense. Classify by what the account is, then the debit/credit rules follow.

Contra accounts: the standard exception

A contra account is paired with another account and carries the opposite normal balance, because its job is to reduce its partner without erasing the partner's recorded history. The classic example is Accumulated Depreciation, a contra-asset: Equipment keeps showing its original cost (debit-normal), and Accumulated Depreciation piles up credits alongside it. The balance sheet reports the net "book value" (cost − accumulated depreciation), but the reader can still see both the original cost and how much has been used up. Other examples: Allowance for Doubtful Accounts (contra to Accounts Receivable) and Sales Returns & Allowances (contra to Sales Revenue, hence debit-normal). The rule of thumb: a contra account always takes the normal balance opposite to the account it offsets.

To balance a T-account: total each side (these subtotals are called footings), subtract the smaller footing from the larger, and write the difference on the larger side as the ending balance. Above: debits foot to 9,200, credits to 3,200, so the ending balance is 6,000 on the debit side — exactly where an asset's balance should sit. If an account's balance lands on its abnormal side (a credit balance in Cash, say), that's usually a red flag: either an error, or something unusual like an overdrawn bank account.

Journal-entry form: the notation

Before amounts are posted into T-accounts, each transaction is first written as a journal entry — accounting's standard notation for "what happened." The conventions:

  • Date first.
  • Debited account(s) listed first, flush left, with the amount in the debit column.
  • Credited account(s) indented, with the amount in the credit column.
  • A short explanation underneath.
  • Total debits must equal total credits — within each entry.
Mar 3  Equipment30,000
Cash12,000
Accounts Payable18,000
Purchased roasting equipment; paid part cash, rest on account.

An entry with more than two accounts — like this one — is a compound entry. It's still one transaction and still balances: 30,000 of debits against 12,000 + 18,000 of credits.

What balancing catches — and what it can't

"Debits equal credits" is a necessary condition for correct books, not a sufficient one. Errors it cannot detect:

  • Right amount, wrong account. Debit Rent Expense instead of Repairs Expense, or debit Cash instead of Accounts Receivable — the entry still balances perfectly, but the accounts are wrong.
  • Omitted or duplicated entries. Skip a transaction entirely, or record it twice, and debits still equal credits.
  • Consistent wrong amounts. Record a $890 payment as $980 on both sides — balanced, and wrong.

Errors it does expose — by making total debits ≠ total credits — include posting only one half of an entry, or recording different amounts on the two sides. One classic diagnostic: a transposition error (writing 980 for 890, swapping adjacent digits) creates a difference that is always evenly divisible by 9 (980 − 890 = 90). A slide (1,000 recorded as 100) also produces a difference divisible by 9. If your books are out of balance and the difference divides by 9, hunt for a transposed or slid number first. The full apparatus for finding and proving these errors — the trial balance — is lesson 101.6; here, the point is simply that the equality check is a smoke detector, not a building inspection.
