101.6

The accounting cycle: journal → ledger → trial balance

Financial statements don't appear by magic — every number on them rode a paper trail from source document to journal to ledger, and the trial balance is the checkpoint that proves the arithmetic survived the trip.

Learn

In 101.5 you learned the grammar of double-entry: every transaction is expressed as debits and credits that must be equal. The accounting cycle is the sentence structure built from that grammar — the standardized sequence of steps a business repeats every accounting period to turn raw transactions into financial statements. This lesson covers the front half of the cycle in full working detail: analyzing transactions, journalizing them, posting to the ledger, and proving the books with an unadjusted trial balance.

The whole cycle at a glance

Most textbooks present the cycle as nine steps. The first four happen continuously during the period (or at least monthly); the rest happen at the end of the period:

  1. THIS LESSONAnalyze transactions from source documents — decide whether, what, and how much to record.
  2. THIS LESSONJournalize — record each transaction chronologically in the general journal.
  3. THIS LESSONPost — transfer journal debits and credits into the individual general-ledger accounts.
  4. THIS LESSONPrepare the unadjusted trial balance — list all account balances and prove debits = credits.
  5. → 101.8Journalize and post adjusting entries — accruals, deferrals, depreciation.
  6. → 101.8Prepare the adjusted trial balance.
  7. → 101.9Prepare the financial statements — income statement, statement of retained earnings, balance sheet, cash-flow statement.
  8. → 101.9Journalize and post closing entries — zero out revenues, expenses, and dividends into retained earnings.
  9. → 101.9Prepare the post-closing trial balance — prove the books are ready for the next period.

Then the cycle starts over. The discipline of the sequence is the point: statements prepared from books that haven't been posted and proved aren't statements, they're guesses.

Step 1 — Analyze the transaction

Accounting doesn't begin with a journal entry; it begins with a source document — the original evidence that an economic event happened. Typical source documents:

  • Sales invoice — you billed a customer (revenue earned on account).
  • Purchase invoice / vendor bill — a supplier billed you.
  • Cash register tape, receipt — cash sale or cash payment evidence.
  • Check stub / remittance advice, bank statement — cash moved.
  • Promissory note, loan agreement — formal debt created.
  • Time cards, payroll records — wages owed or paid.

For each document, transaction analysis asks three questions, in order:

  1. Is this recordable? A transaction is recorded only if it affects the accounting equation (A = L + E) and can be reliably measured in dollars. Signing a contract for future services, hiring an employee, or receiving a price quote changes no asset, liability, or equity yet — no entry. The entry comes later, when cash or performance actually moves.
  2. Which accounts are affected, and do they increase or decrease? Name the specific accounts from the chart of accounts — "Cash" and "Unearned Revenue," not "money" and "stuff we owe."
  3. Debit or credit? Apply the rules of 101.5: debits increase assets, expenses, and dividends; credits increase liabilities, equity, and revenues. Total debits must equal total credits, so the equation stays balanced automatically.

Step 2 — Journalize in the general journal

The general journal is the book of original entry: a chronological record where each transaction first enters the books, complete in one place — date, accounts, amounts, and a short explanation. The standard format has firm conventions:

  • Date in the first column (year and month written once per page).
  • Debited account(s) first, flush left, with amounts in the Debit column.
  • Credited account(s) next, indented, with amounts in the Credit column.
  • A brief explanation below the entry, often citing the source document ("Invoice 1001").
  • A posting-reference (PR) column, left blank at journalizing time — it gets filled in during posting (Step 3).
  • A blank line between entries; no running totals — the journal records, the ledger accumulates.

An entry with more than one debit or more than one credit is a compound entry — still one transaction, still equal totals. Example: buy $9,600 of equipment, paying $3,600 cash with the rest on account:

DateAccount Titles and ExplanationPRDebitCredit

One debit of 9,600 against credits of 3,600 + 6,000 = 9,600. Equal — always.

Step 3 — Post to the general ledger

The journal answers "what happened on May 3?" but it cannot answer "how much cash do we have?" — the Cash pieces are scattered across many entries. Posting fixes that: each debit and credit line in the journal is copied into its own account in the general ledger, the complete collection of accounts.

The chart of accounts

The ledger's table of contents is the chart of accounts: every account the business uses, with an identifying number. The near-universal numbering convention assigns a leading digit per category:

Gaps are left in the numbering so new accounts can slot in without renumbering. Accounts appear in the ledger — and later on the trial balance — in chart-of-accounts order.

The posting mechanics and the audit trail

To post one journal line: find the account's ledger page, enter the date, the amount on the correct (debit or credit) side, and in the account's reference column write the journal page it came from (e.g. J1). Then go back to the journal and write the account's number in the PR column. That two-way cross-reference is the audit trail: from any ledger amount you can trace back to the full journal entry and its explanation; from any journal entry you can confirm both sides were posted. A filled PR column also marks how far posting has progressed — an unfilled PR means an unposted line.

Step 4 — The unadjusted trial balance

At the end of the period (or any time you want a check), you prepare the unadjusted trial balance: a two-column list of every ledger account and its ending balance — debit balances in the left column, credit balances in the right — in chart-of-accounts order, with each column totaled. "Unadjusted" because it's taken before the period-end adjusting entries of 101.8.

What equal totals prove: that the debits and credits you posted are arithmetically equal — no one-sided postings, no addition slips in the balances, no transcription errors onto the trial balance itself.

What equal totals do NOT prove. The trial balance is a parity check, not a correctness proof. It stays perfectly balanced when:

  • a transaction was never journalized at all (both sides missing),
  • an entry was journalized or posted twice (both sides doubled),
  • the right amount was posted to the wrong account (debit Equipment instead of debit Supplies),
  • the wrong amount was used on both sides of an entry,
  • two independent errors offset each other.

When it doesn't balance: the standard hunt

If the columns disagree, compute the difference and work this checklist before re-tracing everything:

  1. Re-add both columns. The most common error is in the trial balance itself.
  2. Difference equals a journal amount? One side of that entry was probably never posted (an omitted posting). Search the ledger for the missing half.
  3. Difference divisible by 2? Divide it by 2 and search for that amount posted to the wrong side — a debit recorded as a credit overstates one column and understates the other, throwing the totals off by twice the amount.
  4. Difference divisible by 9? Suspect a transposition (adjacent digits swapped: writing 1,510 for 1,150) or a slide (decimal shifted: writing 115 for 1,150). Swapping adjacent digits changes a number by 9 × (difference of the digits) × (place value), and a slide changes it by a multiple of 9 as well — so a difference evenly divisible by 9 is the classic fingerprint of both.
  5. If all else fails, re-verify each ledger balance, then re-trace each posting against the journal — the audit trail exists for exactly this.
Why 9? Swap the digits ab (value 10a + b) to ba (value 10b + a) and the change is 9(a − b). At the tens/hundreds place the change is 90(a−b), and so on — always a multiple of 9. A slide divides or multiplies by 10, and n × 10 − n = 9n, again a multiple of 9.

An error hunt, worked

Suppose that while copying balances onto the trial balance, the bookkeeper wrote Supplies as 1,510 instead of 1,150. The debit column now totals 47,210 against credits of 46,850 — out of balance by 360. Run the checklist: 360 isn't a journal amount; 360 ÷ 2 = 180 appears nowhere; but 360 ÷ 9 = 40 — a whole number, the transposition fingerprint. So scan each trial-balance figure against its ledger balance looking for swapped digits, and there it is: 1,510 vs 1,150. (Check the algebra: the swap happened in the hundreds and tens places, digits 1 and 5, so the error is 9 × (5−1) × 10 = 360. Exactly the difference.) Fix it, re-add, balanced.

The road ahead

A balanced unadjusted trial balance is mile-marker four of nine. The balances are arithmetically proven but not yet economically complete: rent that's been used up, supplies consumed, revenue earned but unbilled — none of that is in the books yet. 101.7 builds the accrual-accounting case for why those adjustments are needed; 101.8 journalizes the adjusting entries and re-proves with an adjusted trial balance; 101.9 prepares the statements, closes the temporary accounts, and proves once more with the post-closing trial balance — completing the cycle.
