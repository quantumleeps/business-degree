101.7

Accrual vs. Cash Basis; Revenue Recognition

When you record a sale is a more consequential question than how big it is — accrual accounting exists because cash timing lies about performance, and revenue recognition is where most accounting frauds live.

Learn

1. Two answers to “when did it happen?”

Every transaction has two dates that may differ: when the economic event occurs, and when cash moves. The two accounting bases pick different dates:

  • Cash basis — record revenue when cash is received, expenses when cash is paid. Simple, objective, and how your checking account already thinks.
  • Accrual basis — record revenue when it is earned (the performance obligation is satisfied) and expenses when they are incurred (the benefit is consumed), regardless of cash. This is what GAAP and IFRS require for external reporting; cash basis survives mainly in very small businesses and some tax contexts.

The case for accrual is the periodicity problem: businesses run continuously but must report in slices (months, quarters, years). Cash timing scatters a single economic story across periods — a December sale collected in January makes December look worse and January better than either really was. Accrual accounting reassembles the story: revenues in the period earned, and — by the expense recognition (matching) principle — the expenses that produced them in the same period.

Worked example — same quarter, two verdicts

Quarter 1 (Jan–Mar) at a consulting firm:

  • Jan 1: collects $12,000 in advance for a four-month engagement (Jan–Apr).
  • Feb 1: pays $3,600 for a 12-month insurance policy (Feb–Jan).
  • March: bills a client $30,000 for March work; payment arrives in April.
  • Pays staff $8,000 cash per month, each month, for that month's work.
Cash basisAccrual basis
Advance engagement+12,000+9,000 (3 of 4 months earned)
March billing0 (cash in April)+30,000 (earned in March)
Insurance−3,600−600 (2 of 12 months used)
Staff wages−24,000−24,000 (paid as incurred)
Quarterly income−15,600+14,400

One firm, one quarter: a $15,600 loss or a $14,400 profit depending on the basis. The accrual figure is the honest performance report; the cash figure is an honest liquidity report. You need both — which is why the statement of cash flows (101.4) exists alongside the accrual income statement.

2. The bridge accounts

Whenever recognition and cash part company, a balance sheet account holds the difference until they reunite:

TimingBridge accountType
Revenue earned before cashAccounts receivableAsset (a claim to cash)
Cash received before revenue earnedUnearned (deferred) revenueLiability (an obligation to perform)
Expense incurred before cash paidAccrued liability (e.g., wages payable)Liability
Cash paid before expense incurredPrepaid expenseAsset (future benefit)

Note the counterintuitive one: unearned revenue is a liability, not revenue. Taking a customer's cash creates an obligation to deliver — you owe either the service or the money back. The next lesson (101.8) covers the period-end adjusting entries that move balances across these bridges.

3. The revenue recognition principle — and the five-step model

The core rule: recognize revenue when (or as) the performance obligation is satisfied — when control of the good or service passes to the customer — in the amount you expect to be entitled to. The current standard (ASC 606 / IFRS 15) operationalizes this in five steps:

  1. Identify the contract with the customer.
  2. Identify the performance obligations — the distinct promises in it.
  3. Determine the transaction price.
  4. Allocate the price to the obligations, in proportion to their standalone selling prices.
  5. Recognize revenue when (or as) each obligation is satisfied — at a point in time (delivery of a good) or over time (a subscription, a support contract).
Worked example — a bundled sale

A software firm sells a perpetual license bundled with one year of support for $720. Sold separately, the license goes for $600 and support for $200 (standalone total $800).

Allocate by relative standalone prices: license = 720 × 600/800 = $540, recognized at delivery (point in time); support = 720 × 200/800 = $180, recognized at $15/month over the year (over time). After three months the firm has recognized 540 + 45 = $585, with $135 still sitting in unearned revenue.

Standard cases to know on sight: a magazine subscription collected up front is recognized month by month; a gift card is a liability until redeemed (with breakage — expected never-redeemed value — recognized per pattern of redemption); a long-term construction contract typically recognizes over time by progress toward completion.

4. Matching: three ways expenses attach to periods

  • Direct (cause-and-effect) matching — cost of goods sold is recognized in the same period as the sale it enabled.
  • Systematic allocation — assets serving many periods (equipment, prepaid insurance) are expensed over their useful lives: depreciation and amortization (mechanics in 101.8).
  • Immediate recognition — period costs with no traceable future benefit (most advertising, administrative salaries) are expensed as incurred.

Why this is the fraud chapter: because revenue timing is judgment, it is manipulable — recognizing shipments early (“channel stuffing”), recording sales with side agreements to return goods, or booking long-term contracts up front. The discipline of asking which performance obligation was satisfied, and when? is the auditor's first line of defense, and yours as a reader of statements (101.10 returns to the warning signs).
