101.8

Adjusting Entries: Accruals, Deferrals, Depreciation

Between the trial balance and honest financial statements stands one disciplined ritual: the period-end entries that drag every account from cash timing to economic timing.

Learn

1. Why the books need adjusting

During the period, bookkeeping records transactions as they visibly happen — cash paid, invoices issued. But by period end, time itself has changed the economics: insurance has quietly been consumed, wages have been earned but not paid, customer advances have been partly worked off, equipment has aged. Adjusting entries, made at the end of each accounting period, update the accounts so revenues sit in the period earned and expenses in the period incurred (101.7's accrual principle, executed).

Two structural facts about every adjusting entry:

  • It always pairs one income statement account with one balance sheet account — that's what “moving the economics into the right period” mechanically means.
  • It never touches Cash. Cash already moved (or will move) on its own date; the adjustment fixes recognition, not money.

2. The four types — one 2×2

Classify by two questions: revenue or expense? And did cash move before or after recognition? Cash-first items are deferrals (the entry defers recognition until earned/used); recognition-first items are accruals.

Worked example — one year-end, four adjustments (Dec 31)

(a) Prepaid insurance. Paid $2,400 on Oct 1 for 12 months. By Dec 31, 3 months (3/12 × 2,400 = $600) are used:

Dr Insurance expense 600
Cr Prepaid insurance 600

Prepaid insurance now shows the $1,800 of coverage still owned.

(b) Unearned revenue. Collected $9,000 on Dec 1 for a 3-month service contract; one month is delivered:

Dr Unearned service revenue 3,000
Cr Service revenue 3,000

(c) Accrued wages. Employees earned $1,500 in the last days of December, payable Jan 5:

Dr Wages expense 1,500
Cr Wages payable 1,500

(d) Depreciation. A delivery truck (below) contributes $500/month:

Dr Depreciation expense 500
Cr Accumulated depreciation — truck 500

3. Depreciation: allocation, not valuation

A long-lived asset is a bundle of future service paid for up front; depreciation spreads that cost over the periods served. It is not an attempt to track market value. Straight-line, the default:

annual depreciation = (cost − salvage value) / useful life

The truck: cost $36,000, salvage $6,000, life 5 years → (36,000 − 6,000)/5 = $6,000/year ($500/month). The credit goes not to the asset but to accumulated depreciation, a contra-asset shown beneath the asset, so readers always see both the original cost and how much has been allocated. Book (carrying) value = cost − accumulated depreciation: after 2 years, 36,000 − 12,000 = $24,000 — and it floors at salvage, never below.

An accelerated alternative you'll meet in practice: double-declining balance applies twice the straight-line rate (2/life) to each year's book value, front-loading expense onto the asset's most productive years — total depreciation over the life is the same; only the shape differs.

4. What breaks if you skip an adjustment

Every omitted adjustment misstates both statements at once — by exactly the entry's amount. The reasoning pattern (worth practicing until automatic): name the two accounts the entry would touch; whatever wasn't recorded is over- or understated accordingly.

5. Where this sits in the cycle

From 101.6's cycle: journalize → post → unadjusted trial balance → adjusting entries → adjusted trial balance → financial statements (101.9 closes the loop). The adjusted trial balance is the direct source of the statements — which is why auditors and exam graders care so much about this step: every error here flows straight into what the world sees.

See it: the four adjustments, anatomized

Pick a type. The timeline shows cash vs. recognition; the entry and T-accounts show exactly which income-statement account pairs with which balance-sheet account. Note that Cash appears on no entry.

See it: depreciation paths

Set cost, salvage, and life. Straight-line walks book value down a ramp; double-declining dives early then flattens — both land on salvage, allocating the identical total. The yearly expense table updates live.

See it: what skipping an adjustment does to the statements

Uncheck adjustments to “forget” them at year-end. The statements recompute against the correct baseline — watch net income, assets, liabilities, and equity each misstate by exactly the skipped amounts.
