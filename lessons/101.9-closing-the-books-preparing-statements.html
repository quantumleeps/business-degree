101.9

Closing the Books & Preparing Financial Statements

Every period the income statement's accounts must die and be reborn at zero — closing is how their lifetime of activity becomes a permanent entry in retained earnings, and how the four statements lock together.

Learn

1. Temporary vs. permanent accounts

The ledger splits into two populations with different lifespans:

  • Temporary (nominal) accounts — revenues, expenses, and dividends (withdrawals). They measure activity for one period and must restart at zero each period, or next year's income statement would report this year's sales again. “Net income for 2026” only means something if the meters were zeroed on January 1.
  • Permanent (real) accounts — every balance sheet account: assets, liabilities, and equity (including Retained Earnings). They carry their balances forward forever; the balance sheet is a running total since the company's birth.

Closing entries, made after the adjusted trial balance and the statements, do two jobs at once: reset every temporary account to zero, and transfer its lifetime-of-the-period contents into Retained Earnings, the permanent account that accumulates all earnings ever kept.

2. The four closing entries

The traditional procedure routes everything through Income Summary — a scratch account that exists for one day a year, holding net income while it's in transit:

  1. Close revenues: debit each revenue account (zeroing its credit balance), credit Income Summary.
  2. Close expenses: credit each expense account (zeroing its debit balance), debit Income Summary.
  3. Close Income Summary: its balance now equals net income (credit balance if profitable). Close it to Retained Earnings.
  4. Close Dividends: credit Dividends, debit Retained Earnings — directly, never through Income Summary, because dividends are a distribution of earnings, not an expense of earning them.
Worked example — full close

Adjusted balances at Dec 31: Service revenue $120,000 (credit); total expenses $85,000 (debit); Dividends $10,000 (debit); Retained Earnings, beginning $40,000 (credit).

1) Dr Service revenue 120,000
Cr Income summary 120,000
2) Dr Income summary 85,000
Cr (various) expenses 85,000

Income Summary now holds a credit of 120,000 − 85,000 = $35,000 = net income. Then:

3) Dr Income summary 35,000
Cr Retained earnings 35,000
4) Dr Retained earnings 10,000
Cr Dividends 10,000

Ending Retained Earnings = 40,000 + 35,000 − 10,000 = $65,000 — exactly what the statement of retained earnings reports: beginning RE + net income − dividends = ending RE.

After closing, a post-closing trial balance is prepared. It contains only permanent accounts — if any revenue, expense, or dividend account still shows a balance, a closing entry was missed.

3. Statement preparation: the order is forced

The statements are prepared from the adjusted trial balance in a sequence dictated by who feeds whom:

  1. Income statement — revenues − expenses = net income.
  2. Statement of retained earnings — needs net income from step 1.
  3. Balance sheet — needs ending RE from step 2 to make A = L + E balance.
  4. Statement of cash flows — explains the change in the cash account between balance sheets (101.4).

This chaining is called articulation: the statements aren't four reports, they're one model viewed from four angles, sharing numbers at the seams. Net income appears on the income statement and inside the retained-earnings bridge; ending RE appears on that statement and in the balance sheet's equity; ending cash appears on the balance sheet and as the bottom line of the cash flow statement. Lesson 101.10 uses those seams to audit a real filing.

4. Presentation formats worth knowing

The classified balance sheet sorts assets and liabilities by liquidity: current assets (cash within a year — cash, receivables, inventory, prepaids) before long-term (PP&E net of accumulated depreciation, intangibles); current liabilities (due within a year) before long-term debt. The current/current pairing feeds the current ratio, a first solvency check.

The multi-step income statement computes profit in stages, each a meaningful margin: net sales − cost of goods sold = gross profit; − operating expenses = operating income; ± non-operating items (interest, gains) − tax = net income. A single-step statement lumps all revenues against all expenses — same bottom line, less diagnostic power.

5. The accounting cycle, complete

You now hold the whole loop from 101.5–101.9: (1) analyze transactions → (2) journalize → (3) post to ledger → (4) unadjusted trial balance → (5) adjusting entries → (6) adjusted trial balance → (7) prepare statements → (8) closing entries → (9) post-closing trial balance → (10) next period begins at zero. Steps 5 and 8 are the two places judgment and discipline enter; everything else is mechanical.
