Demand: the curve, shifts, and determinants

"What happens to sales if we raise the price?" is half of every pricing meeting. The demand curve is the disciplined way to answer it — and to keep that answer separate from everything else that moves sales.

Learn

Demand is the buyer side of a market. This lesson builds the demand curve from individual willingness to pay, states the law of demand and why it holds, and then draws the single most-tested distinction in introductory economics: a movement along the curve versus a shift of the curve.

What demand is (and isn't)

Demand is the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity buyers are willing and able to purchase per period, holding everything else constant. Three parts of that definition carry weight:

  • Willing and able — wanting a Tesla isn't demand; wanting one at a price you can and would actually pay is.
  • Per period — demand is a flow: units per week, per month. "Quantity demanded" with no time unit is meaningless.
  • Holding everything else constant — the famous ceteris paribus. The curve isolates the price–quantity relationship; all other influences are frozen and, when they change, move the whole curve.

A demand schedule is the relationship as a table; the demand curve is the same data plotted with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal (a convention from Alfred Marshall that economics has kept ever since).

The law of demand, and why it holds

The law of demand: when the price of a good rises, quantity demanded falls, ceteris paribus — demand curves slope downward. Two forces produce it:

  • The substitution effect. When coffee gets pricier relative to tea, some buyers switch to tea. The relative price change pushes buyers toward substitutes.
  • The income effect. A higher price shrinks the purchasing power of a given budget — buyers can simply afford less of everything, including this good.

A third way to see it: read the curve vertically. At each quantity, the curve's height is the maximum some buyer will pay for that unit — the marginal willingness to pay. Buyers ranked from most-eager to least-eager form a descending staircase, so lower prices reach buyers with lower willingness to pay: more units sell. (This vertical reading is what makes consumer surplus and elasticity work later in the course.)

Demand as an equation

For computation we usually use a linear demand function:

Qd = a − bP     (demand)   |   P = a/b − Qd/b     (inverse demand)

Here a is the quantity buyers would take at a price of zero (the horizontal intercept), and b tells how many units of quantity are lost per dollar of price increase. The price that drives quantity to zero — the vertical intercept a/b — is the choke price.

Worked example. Suppose monthly demand for a meal-kit subscription in one city is

Qd = 500 − 25P
  • At P = $8: Qd = 500 − 25(8) = 300 subscriptions.
  • Raise price to $12: Qd = 500 − 25(12) = 200. The $4 increase costs 25 × 4 = 100 subscriptions — a movement along the curve.
  • Choke price: 500/25 = $20. At $20 or more, no one subscribes.

Movement along vs. shift of the curve

This distinction does more work than any other in the course, so the vocabulary is strict:

  • A change in the good's own price causes a change in quantity demanded — a movement along a fixed curve. Nothing about buyers' circumstances changed; they're just responding to price.
  • A change in anything else buyers care about causes a change in demand — the entire curve shifts, because the quantity demanded is now different at every price. In the equation, the intercept a moves.
If you can say "at the same price as before, buyers now want more (or less)," the curve shifted. If the only thing that changed is the price tag, you moved along it.

The determinants: what shifts demand

Five standard shifters, each worth knowing with its direction:

DeterminantChangeEffect on demand
Income Rises, good is normalIncreases (shifts right) — most goods
Rises, good is inferiorDecreases (shifts left) — e.g. instant noodles, bus rides; buyers trade up
Prices of related goods Price of a substitute rises (tea ↑ for coffee)Increases — buyers switch toward this good
Price of a complement rises (printers ↑ for ink)Decreases — the bundle got pricier
Tastes & preferencesShift toward the good (trend, health study, marketing)Increases
ExpectationsBuyers expect higher future prices or higher future incomeIncreases today — purchases get pulled forward
Number of buyersMarket grows (demographics, market entry, exports open up)Increases — market demand sums over more buyers

Note what is not on the list: the good's own price. It never shifts the curve — it's the axis the curve is drawn against.

From individual to market demand

Market demand is the horizontal sum of individual demands: at each price, add up the quantities (not the prices!) each buyer would take.

Algebraically: for $10 < P ≤ $12 only Liam buys, so market demand is Q = 12 − P. For P ≤ $10 both buy: Q = (20 − 2P) + (12 − P) = 32 − 3P. The market curve has a kink at $10 where Maya enters — real market demand curves flatten as price falls partly because each price cut recruits new buyers, not just more purchases from existing ones.

This is also why "number of buyers" shifts demand: more buyers means more individual curves in the sum at every price.
