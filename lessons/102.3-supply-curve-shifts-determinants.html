102.3

Supply: the curve, shifts, and determinants

Behind every supply curve is a seller doing arithmetic: "at this price, is the next unit worth producing?" Read supply as marginal cost and the whole producer side of the market snaps into focus.

Learn

Supply is the seller side of a market: the relationship between price and the quantity producers are willing and able to sell per period, ceteris paribus. The structure of this lesson deliberately mirrors 102.2 — law, curve, equation, movement-vs-shift, determinants, market summation — because the two sides of the market are built the same way and meet in 102.4.

The law of supply, and where it comes from

The law of supply: when the price of a good rises, quantity supplied rises, ceteris paribus — supply curves slope upward.

The economic engine behind it is rising marginal cost. A producer expands output as long as the price covers the cost of the next unit (the marginal cost, MC). In the short run, marginal cost typically rises with output: the easiest capacity gets used first — the most productive shifts, the best-suited machines, the cheapest suppliers — and squeezing out additional units means overtime pay, expedited freight, and pressing less efficient equipment into service. So a higher price is exactly what makes additional, costlier units worth producing.

Read the supply curve vertically and it's a marginal-cost curve: at each quantity, the height is the minimum price a seller would accept for that unit. (The full cost theory behind MC comes in 102.7; here we only need that MC rises in the short run.) The gap between the market price and that minimum acceptable price, summed over units, is producer surplus — the seller-side twin of consumer surplus.

Two boundary cases keep the law honest: supply can be nearly vertical in the very short run (a fixed catch of fish, concert seats tonight), and individual firms only start producing once price covers the cost of their first units — which is why supply curves often start at a positive price rather than at the origin.

Supply as an equation

Qs = c + dP     (supply)   |   P = (Qs − c)/d     (inverse supply)

The coefficient d > 0 gives the law of supply its upward slope: each extra dollar of price coaxes out d more units. The intercept c is often negative — that's not a bug, it encodes the minimum price needed before anyone produces at all.

Worked example. Suppose the weekly supply of artisan sourdough loaves across a city's bakeries is

Qs = −100 + 30P
  • Entry price: set Qs = 0 → P = 100/30 ≈ $3.33. Below $3.33 a loaf, no bakery covers the marginal cost of even its first batch; the market supplies nothing.
  • At P = $5: Qs = −100 + 150 = 50 loaves.
  • At P = $9: Qs = −100 + 270 = 170 loaves. The $4 price rise pulled out 30 × 4 = 120 extra loaves — bakeries adding batches, staying open longer, and marginal bakeries entering.

Movement along vs. shift of the curve

The same strict vocabulary as the demand side:

  • A change in the good's own price → change in quantity supplied: a movement along a fixed supply curve.
  • A change in anything else sellers care about → change in supply: the whole curve shifts, because the profitable quantity is now different at every price.
Same test as before: "at the same price as before, would sellers now produce a different amount?" Yes → the curve shifted. The only change is the price tag → movement along.

Directional language: an increase in supply means the curve shifts right/down — more quantity at every price, equivalently every quantity offered at a lower minimum price. A decrease in supply shifts it left/up. (Up means decrease on the supply side — students who memorize "up = more" get burned here; think in quantities at a given price.)

The determinants: what shifts supply

DeterminantChangeEffect on supply
Input pricesWages, flour, energy, freight rise Decreases (left/up) — every unit's marginal cost is now higher
Technology / productivityBetter ovens, process improvements Increases (right/down) — same inputs yield more output, MC falls
Taxes & subsidiesPer-unit tax of $t on sellers Decreases — the curve shifts up by exactly $t: sellers need $t more per unit to net the same; a subsidy shifts it down
Prices of related outputsPrice of an alternative product the same resources could make rises (croissants for a bakery; soybeans for a corn farmer) Decreases — resources migrate to the now-more-profitable substitute in production
ExpectationsSellers expect higher prices soon (storable good) Decreases today — withhold inventory to sell later at the better price
Number of sellersEntry of new firms Increases — market supply sums over more firms

Again, the good's own price is deliberately absent — it never shifts its own curve.

One pairing worth keeping straight: substitutes in production (what else the seller could make with the same resources — corn vs. soybeans) are a supply shifter; substitutes in consumption (what else the buyer could purchase — coffee vs. tea) are a demand shifter. Same word, different side of the market.

From the firm to market supply

Market supply is the horizontal sum of individual firms' supply curves: at each price, add the quantities each firm is willing to produce. Because different firms have different costs, price increases raise market quantity through two channels — existing firms slide up their own curves and higher-cost firms cross their entry threshold and start producing. That entry margin is why market supply tends to flatten (get more price-responsive) at higher prices, and it's why "number of sellers" is a shifter.

Worked example. Two producers: firm A with QA = −20 + 10P (produces when P ≥ $2) and firm B with QB = −60 + 10P (needs P ≥ $6 — a higher-cost producer). At P = $5 only A produces: market Q = 30. At P = $8 both do: Q = (−20 + 80) + (−60 + 80) = 80. The market curve kinks at $6 where B enters.
