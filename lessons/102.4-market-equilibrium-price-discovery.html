102.4

Market equilibrium and price discovery

No committee sets the price of coffee, lumber, or software engineers — yet those prices exist, update daily, and coordinate millions of strangers. This lesson is the mechanism that makes that work.

Learn

102.2 built the buyer side, 102.3 the seller side. Put them in the same market and something remarkable happens: a single price emerges at which buyers' and sellers' plans are mutually consistent. This lesson covers what equilibrium is, how to compute it, why prices move toward it, and how to predict where it goes when conditions change (comparative statics) — the workhorse skill of applied microeconomics.

Equilibrium: where plans agree

The market equilibrium is the price–quantity pair (P*, Q*) at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied:

Qd(P*) = Qs(P*) = Q*

P* is the market-clearing price: every buyer willing to pay it finds a seller, every seller willing to accept it finds a buyer, and nothing is left over. "Equilibrium" means exactly what it says — a state with no built-in tendency to change. At any other price, someone's plans are frustrated, and frustrated traders change their behavior; at P*, no one has a reason to.

Worked example (used throughout this lesson). The weekly market for cold-brew concentrate bottles in one city:

Qd = 120 − 4P     Qs = −30 + 6P

Set them equal and solve:

120 − 4P = −30 + 6P  →  150 = 10P  →  P* = $15
Q* = 120 − 4(15) = 60  (check: −30 + 6(15) = 60 ✓)

Always run the check on the other equation — it catches sign errors instantly.

Off equilibrium: surplus and shortage

What if the price is wrong? Both directions are instructive:

  • Price above P* — say $20: Qs = 90 but Qd = 40. Sellers bring 50 more bottles than buyers want: a surplus (excess supply) of 50. Inventory piles up. Sellers holding unsold stock cut price to move it; as price falls, quantity demanded rises and quantity supplied falls until the gap closes at $15.
  • Price below P* — say $10: Qd = 80 but Qs = 30: a shortage (excess demand) of 50. Shelves empty early, waiting lists form, and frustrated buyers bid more (or sellers, seeing stockouts, raise the posted price); as price rises, the gap closes from both sides.
Surplus at P:  Qs(P) − Qd(P) > 0   |   Shortage at P:  Qd(P) − Qs(P) > 0

Crucially, the correction is a movement along both curves — neither curve shifts. The curves describe behavior at every price; the price is what moves.

Price discovery: how markets find P* without anyone computing it

No one in the cold-brew market solves the algebra. Prices converge on P* through decentralized trial and error, and the signals doing the work are mundane:

  • Inventories and stockouts. Unplanned inventory buildup tells a seller "your price is above what the market clears at"; chronic sellouts and backorders say the opposite. Retailers re-price on exactly these signals.
  • Auctions and order books. Securities and commodity exchanges make the process explicit: standing bids and asks adjust until they cross. The textbook image of an auctioneer calling out prices until excess demand is zero (Walras's tâtonnement, "groping") is a stylized version of this.
  • Negotiation and quotes. In B2B markets, quotes that win too easily drift up; quotes that keep losing drift down.

The deeper point (associated with Hayek): the equilibrium price aggregates dispersed information no single participant holds. A frost in Brazil raises coffee's price worldwide; millions of users economize on coffee without knowing why. The price is the signal and the incentive at once — that is what "the price system coordinates" means, and it's why no central planner with a spreadsheet is needed to balance quantities.

Preview — when price isn't allowed to move. If a law caps price below P* (a ceiling, like rent control) the shortage becomes permanent: queues, waiting lists, and non-price rationing replace the price signal. A floor above P* (like a minimum wage in the labor market) makes the surplus permanent. Full treatment when we get to policy applications; for now, note that the surplus/shortage logic above only self-corrects when price is free to adjust.

Comparative statics: predicting the new equilibrium

The most common applied question: conditions changed — what happens to price and quantity? The method is always the same three steps:

  1. Decide which curve shifts (demand shifter or supply shifter? — the determinant lists from 102.2 and 102.3).
  2. Decide which direction.
  3. Read the new intersection.

The four single-shift cases:

ShiftP*Q*Example
Demand ↑ (right)risesrisesHeat wave → cold-brew demand jumps
Demand ↓ (left)fallsfallsCold snap; tastes move on
Supply ↑ (right)fallsrisesCoffee bean prices drop; brewing tech improves
Supply ↓ (left)risesfallsBean costs spike; sellers exit

Worked single shift. A heat wave shifts demand to Qd = 150 − 4P (supply unchanged):

150 − 4P = −30 + 6P → 180 = 10P → P* = $18, Q* = 78

Price and quantity both rose — the signature of a demand increase. (If instead you observed P up but Q down, a supply decrease did it. Reading the (ΔP, ΔQ) pair backwards to infer which curve moved is a genuinely useful diagnostic.)

When both curves shift, one variable is predictable and the other depends on the relative sizes of the shifts — it is ambiguous until you know magnitudes:

CombinationP*Q*
D ↑ and S ↑ambiguousrises
D ↑ and S ↓risesambiguous
D ↓ and S ↑fallsambiguous
D ↓ and S ↓ambiguousfalls

Don't memorize the table — reconstruct it. Each shift "votes" on P and on Q: a demand rise votes P↑ Q↑; a supply rise votes P↓ Q↑. Where the votes agree, the direction is certain; where they conflict, magnitudes decide.
