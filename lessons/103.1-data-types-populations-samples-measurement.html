103.1

Data Types, Populations vs. Samples, Levels of Measurement

Every statistic you will ever compute is only as meaningful as the data behind it — knowing what kind of data you hold, and who it actually represents, is the difference between analysis and noise.

Learn

1. Cases and variables: the shape of a dataset

Statistics starts with a rectangular idea: a dataset is a table in which each row is a case (also called an observational unit or element — a customer, an invoice, a store-month) and each column is a variable — a characteristic measured on every case. Before any computation, a statistician asks two questions: what are the cases? and what are the variables?

2. Qualitative vs. quantitative; discrete vs. continuous

A qualitative (categorical) variable places each case into a category: region, payment method, bond rating. A quantitative (numerical) variable records a quantity for which arithmetic is meaningful: monthly spend, delivery time, number of returns.

Quantitative variables subdivide:

  • Discrete — countable values, usually counts: number of returns (0, 1, 2, …), units sold. Between 2 and 3 returns there is nothing.
  • Continuous — any value in an interval, limited only by measurement precision: delivery time, revenue, weight. Between any two values another is possible.

Warning: digits do not make data quantitative. A ZIP code, a customer ID, or a jersey number is stored as a number but identifies a category. The test is whether arithmetic means anything: the average of ZIP codes 10001 and 90210 is 50105.5, which is the middle of nowhere — literally. Such variables are categorical no matter how they are typed in a spreadsheet.

3. The four levels of measurement

S. S. Stevens's classic hierarchy refines this into four levels of measurement. Each level supports everything below it plus one new property, and each new property licenses new statistics.

LevelNew propertyBusiness examplesLegitimate summaries
NominalCategories can be distinguished (= / ≠ only)Region, industry code, payment method, ZIP codeCounts, proportions, mode
OrdinalCategories have a meaningful orderSatisfaction 1–5, bond ratings (AAA > AA > A), job grade+ median, percentiles
IntervalDifferences between values are meaningful; zero is arbitraryTemperature in °F, calendar year+ mean, standard deviation
RatioTrue zero exists, so ratios are meaningfulRevenue, cost, delivery time, head-count+ coefficient of variation, ratios (“twice as much”)

Why the distinctions matter, concretely:

  • Ordinal gaps are not equal. On a 1–5 satisfaction scale, the jump from 4 to 5 need not equal the jump from 2 to 3. Strictly, that makes a mean of ratings questionable — the median is the defensible center. (In practice, business research often treats multi-item Likert scales as approximately interval; know that this is a convention, not a property of the data.)
  • Interval data forbid ratios. 80 °F is not “twice as hot” as 40 °F, because 0 °F is an arbitrary point, not the absence of temperature. Convert to Celsius and the “ratio” changes — a giveaway that it was never meaningful. (Kelvin, with a true zero, is ratio-level.)
  • Ratio data allow everything. $200,000 of revenue genuinely is twice $100,000, because $0 means no revenue at all.

4. Populations and samples; parameters and statistics

The population is the entire collection of cases about which you want a conclusion. A sample is the subset you actually measure. A census measures the whole population — rarely feasible at business scale (costly, slow, sometimes destructive: you cannot crash-test every car).

The vocabulary splits cleanly along that line:

The central drama of inferential statistics (courses 103.8–103.10 and 204) is using a statistic you can see to estimate a parameter you cannot. Two different errors threaten that move:

  • Sampling error — the statistic differs from the parameter just because of who happened to land in the sample. It is unavoidable, random, and shrinks as the sample grows.
  • Bias (systematic error) — the sampling method itself tilts the result in a consistent direction. It does not shrink as the sample grows; a bigger biased sample just estimates the wrong number more precisely. The infamous 1936 Literary Digest poll reached 2.4 million respondents and still called the U.S. presidential election wrong by 19 points, because its mailing lists (car and telephone owners, mid-Depression) skewed wealthy.

5. How samples get drawn

A sampling frame is the actual list of population members from which you draw — e.g., the loyalty-program database standing in for “all customers.” Gaps between frame and population are a first source of bias.

  • Simple random sample (SRS) — every subset of size n is equally likely. The gold standard; everything in inference assumes it unless told otherwise.
  • Systematic sample — pick a random start, then every k-th case, where k = N / n. Convenient for lists and production lines; dangerous only if the list has a cycle that matches k.
  • Stratified sample — divide the population into non-overlapping strata (e.g., regions), then sample randomly within each. Under proportional allocation, stratum h gets nh = n × Nh / N. Guarantees every stratum is represented and usually reduces sampling error.
  • Cluster sample — randomly select whole groups (stores, branches) and measure everyone inside the chosen clusters. Cheap when the population is geographically scattered; statistically noisier than stratifying.
  • Convenience sample — whoever is easy to reach (shoppers near the entrance, your own followers). Voluntary response sample — whoever chooses to respond (online reviews, call-in polls); dominated by strong opinions. Both are biased by construction, and no sample size fixes them.

Beyond selection bias, watch for nonresponse bias (those who answer differ from those who don't) and response bias (question wording, interviewer effects, or social pressure distorting answers).

Worked example

A coffee chain wants the mean monthly spend of its 12,000 loyalty members but can afford to survey 300. Members split by region: West 6,000, South 4,200, East 1,800.

Identify the pieces. Population: all 12,000 members. Parameter sought: μ = true mean monthly spend (unknown, fixed). Sampling frame: the loyalty database. Variable: monthly spend — quantitative, continuous, ratio level (true zero, ratios meaningful).

Stratified design, proportional allocation:

nWest = 300 × 6,000 / 12,000 = 150  |  nSouth = 300 × 4,200 / 12,000 = 105  |  nEast = 300 × 1,800 / 12,000 = 45

The survey runs and yields x̄ = $86.40. That number is a statistic: a different random sample would give a slightly different x̄. The parameter μ is still unknown — x̄ is our estimate of it, and quantifying how far off it might be is exactly where this course is headed.
