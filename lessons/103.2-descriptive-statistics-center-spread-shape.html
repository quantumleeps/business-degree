Descriptive Statistics: Center, Spread, Shape

“Average” hides four different numbers and a dozen judgment calls — choosing the right summary, and knowing what it conceals, is the first real skill of data literacy.

Learn

1. Measures of center: mean, median, mode

The arithmetic mean is the sum of values divided by the count. For a sample of n observations and for a population of N:

x̄ = Σxᵢ / n      μ = Σxᵢ / N

The mean is the balance point: deviations above and below it cancel exactly, Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = 0. That property is also its weakness — a single extreme value tips the balance hard.

The median is the middle value once data are sorted: position (n+1)/2. With even n, average the two middle values. Because only the position of values matters, the median is resistant — outliers barely move it.

The mode is the most frequent value — the only measure of center available for nominal data (the most common payment method), and useful for spotting multiple clusters.

Worked example — resistance

Nine ordinary days of store sales (in $000): 38, 39, 41, 42, 44, 45, 47, 48, 51. Mean = 395/9 ≈ $43.9k; median = $44k. They agree.

Add Black Friday at 325: mean = 720/10 = $72.0k — higher than 9 of the 10 days, describing nobody. Median = (44+45)/2 = $44.5k, barely moved. For skewed business data (income, deal sizes, claim amounts), report the median; if you report the mean, say why.

The weighted mean averages values whose importance differs, weighting each by wᵢ:

x̄w = Σwᵢxᵢ / Σwᵢ

A portfolio that is 60% in a fund returning 5%, 30% in one returning 8%, and 10% in one returning −2% earns 0.60(5) + 0.30(8) + 0.10(−2) = 3.0 + 2.4 − 0.2 = 5.2% — not the unweighted (5+8−2)/3 ≈ 3.7%.

2. Measures of spread

Two datasets can share a mean and be utterly different. Spread measures how far values lie from the center.

  • Range = max − min. Quick, but determined entirely by the two most extreme values.
  • Interquartile range (IQR) = Q₃ − Q₁, the span of the middle 50%. Quartiles split sorted data into four parts: Q₁ is the median of the lower half, Q₃ of the upper half. The IQR is resistant — it ignores the tails completely.
  • Variance and standard deviation — the workhorses. Both average the squared deviations from the mean:
σ² = Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / N   (population)     s² = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / (n − 1)   (sample)
σ = √σ²      s = √s²

Why n − 1? Two ways to see it. First, the deviations are not free: they must sum to zero around x̄, so once n − 1 of them are known the last is determined — there are only n − 1 independent pieces of information (degrees of freedom). Second, x̄ sits closer to its own sample than μ does, so squared deviations measured from x̄ are systematically a bit too small; dividing by the smaller n − 1 corrects the underestimate on average, making s² an unbiased estimator of σ².

The standard deviation, being the square root of the variance, returns to the original units (dollars, minutes), which is why it — not the variance — is what gets quoted.

3. Relative position and relative spread

A z-score restates a value as “how many standard deviations from the mean”:

z = (x − x̄) / s

A salesperson booking $86k where the team mean is $74k with s = $8k sits at z = (86 − 74)/8 = +1.5. z-scores make different scales comparable and flag outliers (|z| > 2 is unusual, |z| > 3 rare in bell-shaped data).

Two rules connect s to “how much data lies near the mean”:

  • Empirical rule (bell-shaped data only): about 68% of values fall within ±1s of the mean, 95% within ±2s, 99.7% within ±3s.
  • Chebyshev's theorem (any distribution whatsoever): at least 1 − 1/k² of values lie within ±k standard deviations. k = 2 → at least 75%; k = 3 → at least 88.9%.

The coefficient of variation expresses spread relative to the mean, freeing comparisons from units and scale:

CV = (s / x̄) × 100%

Store A's daily sales: mean $50,000, s $5,000 → CV = 10%. A stock fund's monthly return: mean 0.8%, s 1.2% → CV = 150%. The fund is vastly more variable relative to its typical size, a comparison raw standard deviations ($5,000 vs 1.2 percentage points) cannot make.

4. Linear transformations: what shifts, what scales

Add a constant to every value (a flat $500 bonus): the mean and median shift by that constant; the spread — range, IQR, s — is unchanged, because the values move together. Multiply every value by a constant c (a 3% raise, a currency conversion): center multiplies by c, and so does s. The CV is untouched by multiplication but changed by addition — one more reason it's the right tool for relative comparisons.

5. Shape: what center and spread don't tell you

  • Symmetric: mirror image around the center; mean ≈ median.
  • Right-skewed (positively skewed): long tail toward high values — incomes, deal sizes, insurance claims. The tail drags the mean above the median.
  • Left-skewed (negatively skewed): long tail toward low values — exam scores with a ceiling, time-to-failure of reliable machines. Mean below median.
  • Bimodal: two clusters — almost always a sign that two different groups are mixed in one dataset (weekday vs. weekend traffic, two customer segments). The first response to bimodality is to split the data, not to summarize it.

The mean-vs-median comparison is a quick skew check, reliable for clearly skewed data (use it that way in this course), though textbooks note occasional exceptions in lumpy discrete data.

Check yourself

1. Which measure of center is most resistant to outliers?

2. Six customer-service response times (minutes): 12, 7, 9, 15, 11, 9. What is the median response time?

3. A sample of five daily defect counts: 4, 8, 6, 10, 7. Compute the sample standard deviation s (round to two decimals).

4. A portfolio holds 60% in Fund A (return 5%), 30% in Fund B (return 8%), and 10% in Fund C (return −2%). What is the portfolio's weighted mean return, in percent?

5. Order sizes at a B2B distributor are strongly right-skewed. Which relationship between the mean and median should you expect?

6. Product line A: mean weekly sales $50,000 with s = $5,000. Product line B: mean weekly sales $8,000 with s = $1,200. Which line has greater relative variability?

7. Every employee receives a flat $500 monthly bonus added to salary. What happens to the mean and standard deviation of salaries?

8. A rep closed $86,000 in sales this quarter. Team mean: $74,000; team standard deviation: $8,000. What is the rep's z-score?
