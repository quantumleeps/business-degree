103.3

Data Visualization: Histograms, Boxplots, Scatterplots

A good chart is the fastest statistical computation there is — and a bad one is the fastest way to be confidently wrong. This lesson teaches you to build the three core plots and to read them defensively.

Learn

1. From raw numbers to a frequency distribution

Raw quantitative data is unreadable beyond a dozen values. The first organizing move is a frequency distribution: divide the data's range into non-overlapping classes (bins) of equal width and count how many observations fall in each.

Choosing classes involves two decisions:

  • How many classes (k)? Rules of thumb: between 5 and 20; or Sturges' rule, k ≈ 1 + 3.322·log₁₀(n). For n = 40: k ≈ 1 + 3.322(1.602) ≈ 6.3, so 6 or 7 classes.
  • Class width? width ≈ (max − min) / k, rounded up to a convenient number so the classes cover everything.

From the raw counts you derive the relative frequency (count ÷ n, a proportion) and the cumulative relative frequency (running total — “what share of the data is at or below this class?”).

Worked example — building the table

Daily online orders for 40 days; smallest 19, largest 87. Range = 87 − 19 = 68. Sturges suggests ~6–7 classes; with k = 7, width = 68/7 ≈ 9.7 → round up to 10. Start at 15 for clean limits:

2. Histograms — and why they are not bar charts

A histogram draws the frequency distribution: one bar per class, height = frequency (or relative frequency), and bars touch, because the classes partition a continuous numeric axis — there is no gap between “25–34” and “35–44.”

A bar chart displays categorical data: one bar per category, separated by gaps, and the category order is arbitrary (alphabetical, or sorted by height — a Pareto chart). Confusing the two isn't pedantry: a histogram's shape (skew, modality) is meaningful precisely because the axis is numeric and ordered; shuffle a bar chart's bars and nothing is lost.

Reading a histogram's shape: symmetric vs. right/left-skewed; unimodal vs. bimodal (two humps usually mean two mixed populations — split them); isolated bars far from the rest flag outliers.

Bin width is a judgment call with consequences. Too few bins smear real structure into a single blob; too many shatter the picture into noise. Honest practice: try several widths and report a shape only if it survives.

3. Boxplots: the five-number summary drawn to scale

The five-number summary — min, Q₁, median, Q₃, max — becomes a picture: a box from Q₁ to Q₃ with a line at the median, plus whiskers. The standard (Tukey) recipe flags outliers with fences:

lower fence = Q₁ − 1.5 × IQR     upper fence = Q₃ + 1.5 × IQR

Whiskers extend to the most extreme data values inside the fences; anything beyond is plotted as an individual point — a flagged outlier (worth investigating, not automatically deleting: Black Friday is real).

Worked example — fences

Delivery times: Q₁ = 24 min, Q₃ = 40 min. IQR = 40 − 24 = 16. Upper fence = 40 + 1.5(16) = 40 + 24 = 64; lower fence = 24 − 24 = 0. A 70-minute delivery is plotted as an outlier dot; a 62-minute one is just the end of a long whisker.

Skew shows up in a boxplot as asymmetry: a median pushed toward Q₁ with a long upper whisker says right-skewed. Boxplots earn their keep in side-by-side comparisons — five stores' daily sales as five boxes is an instant comparison of level, spread, and outliers that five histograms can't match for compactness.

4. Scatterplots: two variables at once

A scatterplot puts one quantitative variable on each axis, one point per case — the natural display for questions like “do stores with more foot traffic sell more?” Describe what you see in four parts:

  • Direction — positive (up-right) or negative (down-right) association;
  • Form — linear, curved, clustered;
  • Strength — how tightly points follow the form (previewing the correlation coefficient r, lesson 103.10: sign gives direction, magnitude gives strength, ±1 is a perfect line);
  • Unusual features — outliers, gaps, distinct clusters.

Two cautions. r only measures linear association — a strong U-shaped relationship can have r ≈ 0. And association is not causation: ice-cream sales and drowning deaths rise together because summer drives both. A hidden third variable that drives the two plotted ones is called a lurking (confounding) variable; no plot, however pretty, rules it out — only controlled experiments do.

5. Graph literacy: how charts lie

You will see each of these in real business decks. Name them on sight:

  • Truncated y-axis — starting bars at 300 instead of 0 turns a 4% difference into a towering 2:1 visual ratio. Bar heights encode value; cutting the base breaks the encoding. (Line charts may zoom the axis — they encode change by position, not length — but bars may not.)
  • Area and 3-D distortion — doubling a circle's radius quadruples its area; 3-D pie slices nearest the viewer look bigger. Quantities should map to length or position, the encodings humans judge accurately.
  • Dual y-axes — two series, two independently scaled axes: by stretching either scale you can manufacture any crossing or “correlation” you like.
  • Cherry-picked windows and bins — a time series that starts at the trough; a histogram binned to hide the dip. Ask: what does the picture look like with more context?
  • Pie charts — humans compare angles poorly; beyond 3–4 slices use a sorted bar chart.

The constructive principles are the mirror image: label axes and units, start bar charts at zero, show all the relevant data, and let the data — not the decoration — carry the ink.
