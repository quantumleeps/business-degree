103.4

Probability Fundamentals and Rules

Every forecast, risk premium, quality program, and A/B test rests on a handful of probability rules — and on not confusing the two that everyone confuses.

Learn

1. The language: experiments, sample spaces, events

An experiment is any process with an uncertain outcome: a customer arrives or doesn't, a part passes or fails. The sample space S is the set of all possible outcomes; an event is any subset of S. Probability assigns each event A a number P(A) obeying three basic facts: 0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1; P(S) = 1; and for events that cannot co-occur, probabilities add.

Where do the numbers come from? Three classical answers:

  • Classical — when outcomes are equally likely by symmetry: P(A) = (outcomes in A) / (outcomes in S). Two qualified vendors, one contract drawn by lot: each has P = 1/2.
  • Empirical (relative frequency) — P(A) ≈ the fraction of times A occurred in many repeated trials. If 38 of the last 500 shipments arrived late, P(late) ≈ 38/500 = 0.076. The law of large numbers guarantees the running fraction settles toward the true probability as trials accumulate — it says nothing about short runs.
  • Subjective — a coherent degree of belief when neither symmetry nor repetition applies: “we put 30% on winning this acquisition.” Common and legitimate in business; just label it as judgment.

2. The complement rule and “at least one”

P(A′) = 1 − P(A)

Trivial-looking, but it powers the single most useful trick in applied probability: “at least one” is the complement of “none.” If each of 3 independent components fails inspection with probability 0.05, then

P(at least one fails) = 1 − P(none fail) = 1 − 0.95³ = 1 − 0.857375 ≈ 0.1426

Computing it directly (exactly one, exactly two, exactly three…) takes seven terms; the complement takes one.

3. The addition rule: P(A or B)

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)

Adding P(A) and P(B) double-counts the overlap, so subtract it once. If events are mutually exclusive (cannot both happen, P(A ∩ B) = 0) the rule collapses to simple addition.

Worked example

Among a retailer's customers, 55% hold the store credit card (C), 30% use the mobile app (M), and 20% do both. The share who hold the card or use the app:

P(C ∪ M) = 0.55 + 0.30 − 0.20 = 0.65. (Without the subtraction you'd get 0.85, double-counting the 20% who are in both groups.) And by the complement rule, 35% of customers have neither.

4. Conditional probability: updating on information

P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)     (provided P(B) > 0)

P(A | B) — “the probability of A given B” — shrinks the world to the cases where B happened and asks what fraction of those also have A. Most business probabilities are conditional: P(default | low credit score), P(churn | support ticket filed).

  • Conditional: P(Defective | Y) = 16/200 = 0.08 — an inside cell over its row total. Compare P(Defective | X) = 12/300 = 0.04: supplier Y's defect rate is double X's.
  • Flip the conditioning: P(X | Defective) = 12/28 ≈ 0.429. P(A|B) ≠ P(B|A) — the most consequential confusion in applied probability.

5. The multiplication rule and independence

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B | A)

Events A and B are independent when knowing one tells you nothing about the other: P(A | B) = P(A), or equivalently

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B)   (independence)

Check independence by comparing P(A ∩ B) to P(A)P(B); don't assume it. Sampling without replacement breaks independence: drawing 2 parts from a lot of 20 containing 3 defectives, P(both defective) = (3/20)(2/19) ≈ 0.0158 — the second factor is conditional because the first draw changed the lot.

Mutually exclusive ≠ independent — they are nearly opposites. If A and B are mutually exclusive and both have positive probability, then seeing A happen tells you B certainly didn't: P(B | A) = 0 ≠ P(B). Maximum information transfer — maximal dependence. Independent events, by contrast, must be able to co-occur.

6. Total probability and Bayes' theorem

When a scenario splits into cases B₁, B₂, … (mutually exclusive, covering everything), an overall probability assembles from the cases — the law of total probability — and Bayes' theorem runs a conditional backwards:

P(A) = Σ P(A | Bᵢ) P(Bᵢ)      P(B₁ | A) = P(A | B₁) P(B₁) / P(A)
Worked example — the inspection paradox

A production line runs 2% defective. An automated inspection flags 98% of defective parts (its sensitivity) but also falsely flags 5% of good parts. A part just got flagged — what's the probability it's actually defective?

P(flag) = P(flag | def)P(def) + P(flag | good)P(good) = (0.98)(0.02) + (0.05)(0.98) = 0.0196 + 0.0490 = 0.0686.

P(def | flag) = 0.0196 / 0.0686 ≈ 0.286.

A “98% accurate” test, and yet less than a third of flagged parts are bad — because good parts are so numerous that their 5% false-flag rate produces more flags (0.049) than all the defectives combined (0.0196). The base rate dominates. This same arithmetic governs fraud alerts, medical screening, and spam filters: a rare condition plus an imperfect test means most alarms are false.

7. Counting: when outcomes must be enumerated

Classical probability needs counts of outcomes, and three tools do the counting:

  • Multiplication principle: k₁ choices then k₂ then k₃ → k₁k₂k₃ total. A dashboard with 4 layouts × 3 color themes × 2 currencies = 24 configurations.
  • Permutations (order matters): the number of ways to arrange r of n distinct items is nPr = n!/(n−r)!. Ranking 3 finalists out of 8 candidates: 8·7·6 = 336.
  • Combinations (order doesn't matter): nCr = n! / [r!(n−r)!]. Choosing 3 of 8 projects to fund: 8!/(3!5!) = (8·7·6)/(3·2·1) = 56 — each unordered trio was counted 3! = 6 times among the 336 ordered rankings, so divide it out.
