What Managers Do: Planning, Organizing, Leading, Controlling

Management is the discipline of getting work done through other people — and a century of study has converged on a surprisingly stable description of what the job actually is.

Learn

1. What management is

Management is the process of coordinating people and other resources to achieve organizational goals both effectively and efficiently. The two words are not synonyms, and the distinction does real work:

  • Effectiveness — doing the right things: choosing goals that matter and attaining them. Measured against ends.
  • Efficiency — doing things right: the ratio of output achieved to resources consumed. Measured against means.

The combinations are instructive. A firm can be efficient and ineffective — flawlessly producing a product nobody wants (the classic indictment of late-stage Kodak film operations). It can be effective but inefficient — hitting every target at unsustainable cost. Good management pursues both; in conflicts, effectiveness comes first, because there is no prize for doing the wrong thing cheaply.

2. The four functions: POLC

Henri Fayol's century-old decomposition, refined into today's standard framework, says all managerial work falls into four functions performed continuously, not sequentially:

FunctionCore questionRepresentative activities
PlanningWhere are we going, and how?Setting goals, formulating strategy, forecasting, budgeting, developing plans to coordinate work
OrganizingWho does what, with what authority?Designing jobs and structure, grouping work into units, delegating, allocating resources
LeadingHow do we get willing effort?Motivating, communicating, coaching, resolving conflict, shaping culture, leading change
ControllingAre we on track — and if not, what now?Setting standards, measuring performance, comparing to plan, correcting deviations

The functions form a loop: control results feed the next round of planning. A manager who plans without controlling never learns whether the plan worked; one who controls without planning has no standard to control against.

3. The control process, with numbers

Controlling is the most mechanical of the four and deserves its standard four-step statement: (1) establish a standard from the plan; (2) measure actual performance; (3) compare — compute the variance; (4) take corrective action, revise the standard, or do nothing.

percent variance = (actual − standard) / standard × 100%

Because attention is scarce, managers practice management by exception: set a tolerance band and investigate only variances outside it.

Worked example

A fulfillment center's standard is 250 orders packed per shift, with a ±5% tolerance. Tuesday's shift packs 232.

Variance = (232 − 250)/250 = −18/250 = −7.2%. That breaches the −5% threshold → investigate. The diagnosis matters more than the number: a one-off cause (two packers out sick) calls for no system change; a persistent cause (a new packaging step added 40 seconds per order) means either fix the process or — if the step is genuinely required — revise the standard, because a standard nobody can meet corrodes the whole control system. Wednesday packs 243: variance −2.8%, inside the band → leave it alone. Chasing every small fluctuation (“tampering”) wastes attention and often adds noise.

4. What managers actually do all day: Mintzberg's roles

When Henry Mintzberg shadowed executives, he found not four tidy functions but a fragmented scramble — hundreds of brief episodes a day. He catalogued the work into ten roles in three clusters:

  • Interpersonal — figurehead (ceremonial duties), leader (motivating, staffing), liaison (networking outside the unit).
  • Informational — monitor (scanning for information), disseminator (passing information inward), spokesperson (representing the unit outward).
  • Decisional — entrepreneur (initiating change), disturbance handler (reacting to crises), resource allocator (budgets, time, people), negotiator.

The frameworks reconcile cleanly: POLC describes the purposes of managerial work; Mintzberg describes its texture. Announcing quarterly results to analysts is the spokesperson role in service of the controlling function.

5. Skills by level: Katz

Robert Katz argued managers need three skills, in a mix that shifts with level:

  • Technical — proficiency in the unit's actual work. Dominant for first-line supervisors, who train and troubleshoot daily.
  • Human — working with, motivating, and reading people. Roughly constant in importance at every level — the empirical surprise in Katz's scheme.
  • Conceptual — seeing the organization whole: how units interrelate, how the firm sits in its environment. Dominant at the top, where the job is positioning, not production.

This is why excellent individual contributors fail when promoted without support: the promotion quietly swaps the skill that made them excellent for two they have never practiced. Levels are conventionally cut three ways — first-line managers supervise non-managers; middle managers translate strategy into operations and manage managers; top managers set direction for the whole organization.

6. Efficiency in numbers

Worked example

Plant A produces 5,400 units using 450 labor-hours; Plant B produces 6,200 units using 620 labor-hours. Labor productivity: A = 5,400/450 = 12.0 units/hour; B = 6,200/620 = 10.0 units/hour. B is producing more (more output) yet is less efficient — if B matched A's productivity it would need only 6,200/12 ≈ 517 hours, a saving of 103 labor-hours per period. Efficiency comparisons always normalize output by input; raw output comparisons reward size, not management.
