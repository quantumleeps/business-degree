104.3

Organizational Structure and Design

Structure is strategy made physical: who reports to whom, who decides what, and how wide each manager's reach is — and every choice trades coordination against speed and cost.

Learn

1. The six design decisions

Designing an organization means answering six questions; the standard list:

ElementThe questionThe dial runs from … to …
Work specializationHow finely are tasks divided?broad jobs ↔ narrow repeated tasks
DepartmentalizationOn what basis are jobs grouped?function, product, geography, process, customer
Chain of commandWho reports to whom?clear single line ↔ multiple/dual reporting
Span of controlHow many report to each manager?narrow (2–4) ↔ wide (10+)
CentralizationWhere are decisions made?concentrated at top ↔ pushed to the front line
FormalizationHow rule-governed is work?improvised ↔ standardized by written procedure

Specialization raises proficiency and lowers training cost — until monotony, handoff friction, and absenteeism eat the gains. Chain of command carries two classical principles from Fayol: authority (the right, attached to the position, to direct work) and unity of command (one boss per person). Line authority directs the core work; staff authority (legal, HR, finance) advises and supports it.

2. Span of control: the arithmetic of hierarchy

Span of control is the design element with the most visible cost consequences, because it sets how many layers a given workforce needs. With N front-line workers and a uniform span s, each management level is roughly 1/s the size of the one below, until a single chief remains:

Worked example — what widening the span buys

A firm has 1,024 front-line employees. Average fully loaded manager cost: $120,000.

Span of 4: levels of 256 → 64 → 16 → 4 → 1 managers = 341 managers, 5 layers.
Span of 8: levels of 128 → 16 → 2 → 1 = 147 managers, 4 layers.

Difference: 194 fewer managers ≈ $23.3M/year, plus one fewer layer for every decision and message to traverse. So why not always go wide? Because each manager now coaches 8 instead of 4 — wide spans only work when work is standardized, employees are experienced, and the manager's job is enabling rather than checking. Narrow spans buy close supervision at the price of cost, slower vertical communication, and micromanagement risk. The modern trend is wider spans and flatter pyramids, with formalization and training absorbing what supervision used to do.

3. Departmentalization: five bases for grouping

  • Functional — group by skill (engineering, marketing, finance). Deep expertise and scale economies; weak cross-functional coordination, slow product decisions, “silo” pathology.
  • Product (divisional) — group by product line, each division with its own functions. Clear P&L accountability and product focus; duplicates functions across divisions.
  • Geographic — group by region. Local responsiveness (regulation, taste); duplication plus the risk of regional fiefdoms.
  • Process — group by workflow stage (intake → assembly → finishing). Efficient flow; handoff boundaries.
  • Customer — group by customer type (enterprise, SMB, consumer). Tailored service; duplicated capability per segment.

Large firms mix bases by level — e.g., product divisions at the top, functions inside each division, geography inside sales.

4. The structural forms

  • Simple structure — an owner and everyone else; fast, informal, collapses past ~20–30 people.
  • Functional structure — the functional grouping writ large; the default for single-product firms.
  • Divisional structure — product/geographic divisions as semi-autonomous businesses.
  • Matrix structure — functional and product/project lines simultaneously: an engineer reports to the engineering manager and a project manager. It deliberately violates unity of command to get both deep expertise and project focus; the price is two-boss conflict, negotiation overhead, and stress — manageable only with clear protocols for whose call is whose.
  • Team and network/virtual structures — work organized in cross-functional teams; or a small core firm contracting out manufacture, logistics, even design. Flexibility at the price of control and culture.

5. Mechanistic vs. organic — and what decides

The six dials cluster into two coherent profiles. A mechanistic design: high specialization, rigid departmentalization, narrow spans, high centralization and formalization — a machine for efficiency in repeatable conditions. An organic design: broad jobs, cross-functional teams, wide spans, decentralized decisions, low formalization — built to adapt. Neither is better; the contingency factors decide:

  • Strategy — cost leadership favors mechanistic; innovation favors organic.
  • Size — growth pushes toward mechanistic (you cannot improvise at 10,000 people).
  • Technology — Joan Woodward's classic finding: mass production fit mechanistic structures; unit/custom and continuous-process production fit organic ones.
  • Environment — the headline variable: stable, predictable environments reward mechanistic efficiency; turbulent ones (shifting tech, demand, regulation) demand organic adaptability. Lawrence & Lorsch: the more uncertain the environment, the more decentralized and flexible the better performers.

6. Centralization, delegation, and where decisions should live

Centralization concentrates decisions where the widest view lives; decentralization moves them to where the information is freshest. The practical rule: push each decision to the lowest level that has the information and competence to make it, and keep at the top only what truly requires the whole-firm view (capital allocation, brand, major risk). Delegation is the mechanism — assigning responsibility with matching authority — and its classical failure is delegating responsibility while hoarding authority, which produces accountable people who can't act.
