Decision-Making Models and Biases

Organizations are decision factories — and both the formal machinery (expected value, decision rules) and the predictable ways human judgment breaks are now standard equipment for any manager.

Learn

1. The rational model — and its assumptions

The textbook rational decision-making model runs in steps: (1) identify and define the problem; (2) establish decision criteria; (3) weight the criteria; (4) generate alternatives; (5) evaluate each alternative against the weighted criteria; (6) choose the best; then implement and evaluate. It produces the optimal choice — given assumptions that should make you suspicious: the problem is clear, all alternatives and consequences are known, preferences are stable, and time and cost don't constrain the search.

Herbert Simon's Nobel-winning correction: real decision makers operate under bounded rationality — limited information, limited cognitive capacity, limited time. So they construct simplified models of the problem and satisfice: search until the first alternative that is good enough against their criteria, then stop. Satisficing is not laziness; when search is costly and the environment forgiving, stopping early is itself rational. Managers also lean on intuition — pattern recognition compiled from experience — which performs well precisely where the manager has deep, feedback-rich experience, and treacherously elsewhere.

2. Decision conditions: certainty, risk, uncertainty

Decision theory classifies situations by what you know about the future:

  • Certainty — outcomes of each alternative are known (choosing between two fixed-rate deposits). Rare and easy.
  • Risk — possible outcomes and their probabilities are known or estimable. The standard tool is expected value.
  • Uncertainty — outcomes are known but probabilities are not. Choice then reveals temperament: optimist, pessimist, or regret-minimizer.

3. Deciding under risk: expected value

EV(alternative) = Σ (probability of state × payoff in that state)
Worked example — the launch decision

Launching a product costs money up front; demand may be strong (probability 0.6, payoff +$400k) or weak (probability 0.4, payoff −$150k). The safe alternative — licensing the design — pays a sure +$120k.

EV(launch) = 0.6 × 400 + 0.4 × (−150) = 240 − 60 = +$180k > $120k → launch, if the firm can afford the 40% chance of losing $150k. Expected value is a long-run average over many such bets; a firm that cannot survive the bad branch should weight downside risk beyond its probability (that judgment, formalized, is risk aversion and expected utility — later courses). A useful companion number: the probability p* that makes you indifferent solves 400p − 150(1−p) = 120 → p* = 270/550 ≈ 0.49. The launch decision is robust as long as you believe strong demand is more likely than a coin flip.

Decision trees chain these computations: decision nodes (squares) where you choose, chance nodes (circles) where probability chooses, rolled back from the leaves by expected value.

4. Deciding under uncertainty: three classic rules

With no probabilities, three rules give defensible — and different — answers. Consider three capacity options against two demand states (payoffs in $k):

AlternativeDemand highDemand low
A: Build large plant8020
B: Build small plant6050
C: Subcontract3040
  • Maximax (optimist): best of the best outcomes → A (80).
  • Maximin (pessimist): best of the worst outcomes → worst payoffs are A: 20, B: 50, C: 30 → B.
  • Minimax regret: build the regret table — in each state, regret = (best payoff in that state) − (your payoff). High-demand best is 80, low-demand best is 50: regrets A: (0, 30), B: (20, 0), C: (50, 10). Maximum regrets: A 30, B 20, C 50. Minimize the maximum → B (worst-case regret 20).

5. The biases: predictable failures of judgment

Behavioral research (Kahneman & Tversky and successors) shows judgment errs in systematic, not random, directions. The manager's shortlist:

BiasThe errorBusiness signature
AnchoringFirst number dominates subsequent estimatesOpening offer frames the negotiation; last year's budget frames this year's
ConfirmationSeek and overweight evidence that fits the existing beliefPilot studies designed to succeed; dashboards that only track the happy metric
AvailabilityJudge frequency by ease of recallOverreacting to vivid, recent failures; underpricing quiet chronic risks
RepresentativenessJudge by resemblance to a stereotype, ignoring base rates“He looks like our best rep did” hiring; small-sample pattern reading
OverconfidenceConfidence outruns accuracyProject plans missing their own deadlines (planning fallacy)
Escalation of commitmentIncreasing investment in a failing course because of what's already sunk“We've spent $4M, we can't stop now” — sunk costs are gone either way
FramingSame facts, different choice depending on gain/loss wording“90% retention” sells what “10% churn” scares; risk-seeking under loss frames
HindsightPast events feel predictable after the factPost-mortems that punish defensible bets for unlucky outcomes

The escalation/sunk-cost pair deserves the formal statement: only incremental costs and benefits — those that differ between go-forward options — are decision-relevant. Money already spent is identical across every option and therefore can never justify any of them.

6. Group decisions: better and worse

Groups bring more information and legitimacy, but add groupthink — the suppression of dissent to preserve cohesion (Janis's diagnosis of the Bay of Pigs). Symptoms: illusions of unanimity and invulnerability, self-censorship, pressure on dissenters. Standard remedies are structural, not exhortative: assign a rotating devil's advocate, have the leader withhold their view until last, solicit positions independently and in writing before discussion, invite outside experts, and run a premortem (“it's a year from now and this decision failed — write its history”). Each remedy works the same way: it makes dissent cheap and silence informative.
